# Beeber Boards & Cards — Product Requirements Document

## 1. Executive Summary

The product vision for Beeber Boards & Cards is to fundamentally democratize tabletop game design by providing an end-to-end, AI-powered platform that accelerates the creation process from initial concept to print-ready physical production. The global tabletop games market currently represents an approximately $14.3 billion opportunity, with the independent design and crowdfunding segment growing at an accelerated 9.2% year-over-year rate. Despite this economic expansion, the barrier to entry remains prohibitively high; independent designers typically spend an average of fourteen to eighteen months iterating through disjointed software tools for rules documentation, balance testing, asset layout, and manufacturing preparation. Beeber Boards & Cards addresses this structural friction directly by unifying the workflow. The core value proposition is to reduce the game development lifecycle from months to days by providing a centralized, intelligent workspace that seamlessly integrates natural language concept design, mathematical balance simulation, style-consistent asset generation, and manufacturer-compliant exporting.

## 2. Target Users & Prioritization

The platform categorizes its target audience into primary, secondary, and expansion personas based on their distinct jobs-to-be-done, workflow pain points, and calculated willingness to pay for specialized tooling. The prioritization strategy dictates that core mechanics and export features must serve the commercial intent of the primary persona before optimizing the onboarding experience for the secondary persona.

The primary persona is identified as the **Serious Indie Designer**. This user is actively developing tabletop games with the explicit intent of launching commercial crowdfunding campaigns on platforms such as Kickstarter or Gamefound. They require highly robust, mathematically sound tools capable of handling complex rule sets. Market research indicates that this demographic allocates between $40.00 and $60.00 monthly for specialized design and prototyping software. Their top three jobs-to-be-done revolve around efficiency and mathematical validation. First, they must rapidly prototype and iterate on large decks containing fifty or more unique card instances without the friction of manual spreadsheet data entry. Second, they need to validate their game mechanics and economic balancing using mathematical models prior to organizing physical playtesting sessions, which are logistically expensive. Third, they require the ability to generate print-ready, manufacturer-compliant assets with precise bleed margins and safe zone specifications tailored to specific printing services.

The secondary persona is the **Hobbyist First-Timer**. This user is driven by creative passion rather than commercial intent, often seeking to design a custom game for family game nights or personal enjoyment. They possess creative thematic ideas but fundamentally lack structural knowledge of game mechanics, mathematical probability, and graphic design principles. Their willingness to pay is significantly lower, averaging $10.00 to $15.00 monthly. Their top three jobs-to-be-done involve overcoming knowledge gaps. They need to translate vague thematic concepts into structured, functional game rules. They must overcome "blank canvas syndrome" through guided artificial intelligence prompts and standardized template structures. Finally, they aim to produce a functional Print-and-Play portable document format (PDF) to test their creation immediately.

Expansion personas represent future market opportunities to be pursued post-launch once the core application architecture is stabilized. The **Educator** persona focuses on gamified learning within classroom environments. This demographic is highly valuable because institutional and district-level licenses offer high-margin, low-churn recurring revenue streams. They should be targeted in the second year of operation, concurrent with the development of collaborative sharing features and compliance with educational data privacy standards. The **Publisher** persona represents established tabletop game publishing houses seeking rapid internal prototyping tools for evaluating pitched games. While they possess the highest willingness to pay, they also demand enterprise-grade intellectual property security and custom proprietary asset integration, necessitating a robust enterprise sales motion. The **Corporate** persona utilizes custom game design for organizational team-building or corporate training programs. This segment is highly opportunistic and requires minimal feature adjustments to the core platform, though it benefits significantly from specialized template framing, such as workshop toolkits and icebreaker game presets.

## 3. Product Overview

The Beeber Boards & Cards application architecture is constructed around a linear, yet highly iterative, six-stage pipeline. Users are not strictly bound to a linear progression; the platform is designed with flexible entry points. For instance, a user who has already written a comprehensive rulebook offline can bypass the initial concept stages and inject their data directly into the balancing engine, or a user with existing commissioned artwork can import those assets directly into the layout editor.

### Stage 1: Brief

Upon entering the application, the user interacts with a conversational interface, providing unstructured thematic ideas, desired player counts, and target session lengths. Behind the scenes, the artificial intelligence utilizes natural language processing to analyze the input against a vast taxonomy of established board game mechanics. It then recommends appropriate core gameplay loops, such as worker placement or deck-building, and generates a foundational design document formatted as structured data. The user retains ultimate control, deciding to approve, manually edit, or completely reject the suggested mechanics, thereby finalizing the core premise of the game.

### Stage 2: Design

The user interface transitions from a conversational view to a highly structured, spreadsheet-like data grid where game entities—such as cards, boards, and resource tokens—are strictly defined. The underlying artificial intelligence extrapolates the approved brief into specific card types and instances, autonomously generating card text, resource cost ratios, and in-game effects formatted within a strict schema. The user reviews this generated data, manually adjusting specific attributes, adding new custom instances, or instructing the system to recursively expand a specific faction or card family based on the established patterns. All artifacts generated in the Brief stage directly inform the structural constraints applied here.

### Stage 3: Balance

The user is presented with a comprehensive analytical dashboard featuring statistical charts, win-rate graphs, and turn-length distributions. The backend infrastructure compiles the structured data matrix from the Design stage into an abstract computational state machine. It then executes thousands of automated Monte Carlo simulations to empirically identify dominant strategies, infinite action loops, or disproportionately over-costed cards. The user evaluates the flagged anomalies and adjusts the mathematical parameters, such as reducing a card's damage output or increasing its resource cost. These adjustments seamlessly update the central data model, triggering immediate re-evaluations of the game's mathematical equilibrium.

### Stage 4: Art

The interface shifts to a visual gallery organized by the card instances defined in the Design stage, featuring an integrated prompt editor and visual reference upload zone. Utilizing advanced style-conditioning architectures, the artificial intelligence generates illustrations and iconography that maintain a rigorous stylistic coherence across the entire game ecosystem. This ensures that an asset generated during the initial session matches the aesthetic profile of an asset generated weeks later. The user exercises curatorial control, choosing to regenerate, refine through inpainting, or finalize specific images to be permanently bound to the underlying data entities.

### Stage 5: Layout

The user accesses a visual, drag-and-drop template editor to design a master visual hierarchy for each distinct card type, defining absolute spatial bounding boxes for titles, artwork, descriptive text, and resource icons. The system's rendering engine automatically injects the textual data from the Design stage and the visual imagery from the Art stage into these master templates, instantly rendering the entire catalog of components without requiring manual, card-by-card positioning. The user fine-tunes typographic elements, adjusts individual text scaling rules to prevent visual overflow, and validates the overall legibility of the generated components.

### Stage 6: Export

The user interacts with a final review interface displaying physical print specifications and various destination routing options. The computational engine compiles the finalized graphical layouts into standard industry formats, applying dynamic spatial calculations to generate required crop marks, bleed areas, and color profile conversions based on the selected manufacturing output method. The user makes the final decision to download a Print-and-Play document for immediate localized printing, export a digital package formatted for virtual tabletop simulators, or push the proprietary files directly to manufacturing partners via secure application programming interfaces.

## 4. Feature Specifications (MVP)

The Minimum Viable Product must deliver a fully functional realization of the six-stage pipeline. The following specifications outline the required engineering features, artificial intelligence behaviors, and critical edge cases that must be accommodated to ensure a stable launch.

### 4.1 Natural Language Concept Intake with Mechanic Recommendation

The concept intake feature serves as the top-of-funnel entry point for the application. The primary user story dictates that a Hobbyist First-Timer wants to describe their game idea in unstructured plain text so that the system can mathematically structure it into standard tabletop mechanics and rules. The acceptance criteria require that the text input area accept up to 5,000 characters of unstructured text. The system must output a clearly structured taxonomy that explicitly defines the Core Loop, Win Condition, Player Interaction Type, and Complexity Rating. Furthermore, the user must be able to accept or regenerate specific sub-sections of this recommendation independently without discarding the entire generation.

The artificial intelligence behavior relies on a large language model mapping the unstructured input against a predefined JSON schema of established tabletop game mechanics. The prompt engineering must strictly constrain the model from generating esoteric or highly physical mechanics that fall outside the testing capabilities of the platform's computational Balance engine. In the event of an edge case where a user inputs a fundamentally contradictory prompt—such as requesting a competitive cooperative game where players must eliminate each other to win together—the system must detect the logical fallacy, halt the schema generation, and issue a conversational clarifying prompt asking the user to prioritize their intended interaction model. This feature is designated as a **Priority 0 launch blocker**.

### 4.2 Card Type and Card Instance Generation with Structured Editing

The data generation matrix is the core workflow engine for the Serious Indie Designer persona, who needs the system to autonomously generate large batches of unique game components based on overarching rules to avoid tedious manual data entry. The acceptance criteria mandate that the user interface provide a responsive, spreadsheet-like grid view facilitating the bulk editing of component attributes, including names, resource costs, mechanical effects, and taxonomic types. The generation tooling must support parameterized macro-commands, allowing a user to request the creation of ten low-cost fire-elemental cards. Crucially, all generated attributes must strictly adhere to the internal TypeScript data schema defined in the repository's core types file.

The artificial intelligence behind this feature utilizes strict JSON-mode generation to output extensive arrays of card objects. It must constantly reference the game's foundational Brief parameters stored in the application state to ensure that every generated component aligns with the established thematic and mechanical constraints. A significant edge case occurs when the model hallucinates a mechanical effect that cannot be parsed into the application's internal vocabulary for the Balance engine. When this occurs, the system must highlight the offending spreadsheet cell with a validation warning, requiring manual user correction before the data can be passed to subsequent pipeline stages. This feature is a **Priority 0 launch blocker**.

### 4.3 Monte Carlo Balance Simulation with Visualization Dashboard

The mathematical validation engine allows the Serious Indie Designer to simulate thousands of automated matches of their game, identifying overpowered strategies and dominant component synergies long before committing to physical printing. The feature must support customizable simulation runs ranging from one hundred to ten thousand distinct iterations. The resulting analytical dashboard must render interactive data visualizations showcasing critical metrics such as average turn duration, aggregate win percentages segmented by starting position, and specific card utilization frequencies. The system must automatically surface algorithmic alerts for specific game entities that heavily skew win probabilities beyond acceptable standard deviations.

The technical behavior of this feature heavily relies on translating the structured component data into an executable state machine. Specialized heuristic agents—rather than latency-heavy large language models—must play the game programmatically, executing actions based on assigned behavioral profiles such as aggressive pacing, control-oriented resource hoarding, or pure statistical randomness. The most critical edge case involves infinite loops triggered by recursive card interactions. If a simulated state enters an action loop that exceeds a predefined depth limit, the simulation environment must automatically terminate the specific thread and report the exact causal loop trajectory to the user interface to prevent server exhaustion. This feature is designated as a **Priority 1 launch target**.

### 4.4 Style-Consistent AI Art Generation

The visual asset generation pipeline enables designers to produce artwork for entire catalogs of cards that look cohesively bound to a single artistic universe, elevating the perceived professionalism of the prototype. The user must be able to establish a global style reference using either a textual prompt array or an uploaded reference image. The generation engine must subsequently lock specific character designs, architectural motifs, and color palettes across dozens of distinct image outputs. The interface must also provide a batch generation capability, allowing a user to queue rendering tasks for multiple selected grid rows simultaneously.

To achieve this behavior, the backend infrastructure must orchestrate a diffusion-based generative pipeline utilizing advanced adapter architectures for deep structural and stylistic conditioning. The system must automatically parse the textual effect descriptions and component titles from the Design stage into optimized positive visual prompts, appending the user's global style parameters seamlessly. An operational edge case arises when safety filters are erroneously triggered by standard tabletop fantasy terminology, such as "blood magic" or "sword strike." The middleware must gracefully catch these API rejections and immediately prompt the user with automated, sanitized token substitutions to ensure the batch queue continues processing. This feature is a **Priority 0 launch blocker**.

### 4.5 Template-Based Card Layout Editor

The spatial mapping interface allows the Serious Indie Designer to bind their generated data fields to distinct visual zones on a master template, ensuring that localized rule changes automatically propagate across the entire component library without requiring manual graphical adjustments. The editor must support the absolute pixel-perfect positioning of varied zones, including primary illustrations, textual title boxes, descriptive body copy areas, and dynamic resource cost iconography. Furthermore, the designated text zones must implement advanced auto-scaling typography algorithms to prevent visual overflow when highly verbose card effects are injected. Any mutation to the master template architecture must trigger a real-time re-rendering of all dependent component instances within the user's viewport.

The rendering pipeline relies on highly deterministic layout algorithms rather than generative artificial intelligence, ensuring absolute consistency in output. However, an integrated assistant may offer algorithmic suggestions for optimal typographic pairings or spatial hierarchy structures based on the calculated density of the input data. A critical edge case occurs when a specific card instance contains a volume of text that fundamentally breaks the auto-scaling logic, forcing the font size below acceptable legibility thresholds. In this scenario, the system must explicitly flag the individual card with a high-priority accessibility warning directly within the Layout stage interface, preventing the user from exporting an illegible product. This feature is a **Priority 0 launch blocker**.

### 4.6 Print-and-Play PDF Export

The foundational export feature guarantees that a Hobbyist can immediately download a formatted document of their game, properly scaled for standard A4 or US Letter paper dimensions, facilitating immediate physical cutting and playtesting. The export compilation engine must automatically execute ganging algorithms, efficiently arranging multiple component instances onto single pages to minimize physical paper waste. The user interface must present explicit toggle options allowing the inclusion of crop marks, fold lines, and safe zone boundaries. To ensure an acceptable user experience, the generation and delivery of a one-hundred-card document must be completely resolved within a fifteen-second latency budget.

The behavior of this stage is entirely deterministic, utilizing standard document generation libraries to translate the digital DOM into a vector-based portable document format. An edge case involves projects containing highly irregular or heterogeneous component sizes, such as a game combining large tarot-sized cards with standard mini-euro resource cards. The ganging algorithm must dynamically calculate the most efficient bin-packing strategy across the page dimensions to optimize the layout and prevent overlapping cut lines. This feature is a **Priority 0 launch blocker**.

### 4.7 The Game Crafter Integration

The direct manufacturing integration empowers the Serious Indie Designer to transmit their finalized digital assets directly to a commercial printing facility, completely bypassing the friction of manual file compression and external uploading. The implementation must secure a robust authentication flow with the manufacturer's application programming interface. It must feature automated architectural mapping, translating the platform's internal component schemas into the manufacturer's specific product inventory objects. Additionally, the system must dynamically inject the manufacturer's required physical bleed zones into the final image outputs prior to transmission.

The system programmatically constructs the required multipart payloads and actively manages the asynchronous asset upload queue. The primary edge case involves network latency, rate limiting, or gateway timeouts from the external interface during the transmission of massive, high-resolution image batches. The backend architecture must implement rigorous, fault-tolerant retry logic utilizing exponential backoff algorithms to ensure data integrity without overwhelming the external servers. This feature is designated as a **Priority 1 launch target**.

### 4.8 Project Save/Load and Version History

State persistence and historical versioning allow users to iterate aggressively without the psychological friction of potentially destroying a functional game balance. The acceptance criteria require that the application state be persistently written to a remote database upon every significant mutation within the workspace. Furthermore, the user interface must expose a linear, traversable history of project states, allowing users to define named checkpoints and revert the entire application context to a previous version instantaneously.

The behavior is driven by optimistic UI updates backed by robust remote database synchronization. A complex edge case emerges when a user operates simultaneous sessions across multiple browser tabs, risking severe state collision and data corruption. The underlying architecture must enforce a strict single-source-of-truth policy, utilizing optimistic concurrency control or distributed locking mechanisms to reject conflicting state mutations and prompt the user to refresh their local client view. This feature is a **Priority 0 launch blocker**.

### 4.9 AI Chat Assistant

The contextual support interface provides designers with immediate, hyper-specific assistance directly within the application workspace, mitigating creative blocks and reducing support ticket volume. The interface must manifest as a persistent, collapsible interaction panel accessible across all six stages of the pipeline. Crucially, the assistant must possess deep contextual awareness of the user's specific project state, the precise data they are currently editing, and the specific functional stage they are viewing.

The backend behavior relies on retrieval-augmented generation methodologies. The application actively serializes the current state matrix and specific UI context, injecting it directly into the system prompt of the language model. This enables the assistant to provide highly relevant advice, such as noting that a specific card is statistically underperforming in the Balance stage and suggesting an exact numerical adjustment. An edge case occurs when the serialized project data exceeds the maximum token context window of the designated language model. The infrastructure must implement intelligent truncation protocols, strategically summarizing historical data or pruning unrelated stage metrics before dispatching the payload. This feature is a **Priority 2 fast follow**.

## 5. Non-Functional Requirements

### 5.1 Performance Targets

The platform must adhere to strict latency budgets to maintain the illusion of real-time responsiveness, which is critical for a web-based design tool. Text-based artificial intelligence generation requests within the Brief and Design stages must achieve a time-to-first-token of less than 1,500 milliseconds. Image generation tasks within the Art stage must fully resolve and render to the client within 5,000 milliseconds per asset. The computational execution of one thousand Monte Carlo simulation iterations in the Balance stage must complete within a 3,000-millisecond threshold. Finally, the deterministic PDF export engine must process and gang layouts at a sustained rate exceeding ten pages per second.

### 5.2 Accessibility

The user interface must rigorously adhere to Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.1 Level AA standards. This compliance mandates comprehensive keyboard navigability through the entirety of the six-stage pipeline, ensuring that users with motor impairments can execute complex design tasks without relying on precise cursor movements. Furthermore, the color contrast ratios, particularly within the dense statistical data visualizations of the Balance dashboard, must be meticulously verified to support users with various forms of color vision deficiency.

### 5.3 Security

The application's security posture must prioritize intellectual property protection and strict authentication protocols. API key management for internal routing to proprietary foundational models must be strictly isolated to backend environments; keys must never be exposed within the client-side payload. User authentication must be governed by secure, HTTP-only JSON Web Tokens to prevent cross-site scripting vulnerabilities. Because generated assets remain the intellectual property of the individual users, the storage architecture must enforce strict tenant isolation, utilizing row-level security policies to prevent any possibility of cross-account data leakage.

### 5.4 Scalability

The platform architecture must be provisioned to handle sustained loads of five hundred concurrent, active editing sessions without exhibiting performance degradation. The data persistence layer must accommodate expansive asset storage requirements, supporting programmatic limits of up to two gigabytes of storage for free-tier users and extending up to twenty gigabytes for premium subscriber accounts, necessitating highly elastic blob storage solutions.

### 5.5 Browser and Device Support

The application mandates full functional support across all modern Chromium-based browsers, Mozilla Firefox, and Apple Safari within desktop computing environments. Due to the extreme density of the spreadsheet interfaces within the Design stage and the precise spatial requirements of the Layout stage, comprehensive mobile device support is explicitly excluded from the Minimum Viable Product scope, though responsive graceful degradation should inform the underlying CSS architecture.

## 7. Success Metrics

To objectively evaluate the platform's market penetration and functional utility, specific quantitative telemetry must be established across the application lifecycle. The singular North Star metric is defined as the volume of **"Print-Ready Exports Generated."** This specific action definitively proves that a user has successfully navigated the inherent complexities of the entire six-stage pipeline and successfully extracted tangible, physical value from the digital platform.

The primary **Activation** metric is triggered when a newly registered user successfully completes the Design stage—defined by the algorithmic generation of at least twenty distinct card instances—and subsequently transitions into either the Balance or Art stages for the first time. **Retention** signals will be strictly monitored through the measurement of Weekly Active Users returning specifically to execute subsequent Monte Carlo simulations. The iterative testing loop inherent within the Balance stage is hypothesized to be the primary behavioral predictor of long-term subscription retention. **Financial health** will be tracked via standard Software-as-a-Service revenue metrics, including Monthly Recurring Revenue, Average Revenue Per User, and the specific conversion velocity between the free Apprentice tier and the paid Artisan tier. Finally, **Quality** metrics will analyze the rate of manual cell edits required within the Design stage grid. A disproportionately high manual edit rate serves as a negative signal indicating that the natural language generation prompt is failing to accurately capture the user's intent, signaling an immediate requirement for backend system prompt refinement.

## 8. Risks & Mitigations

The deployment of an end-to-end generative application introduces significant overlapping risk vectors that must be actively managed throughout the development lifecycle.

**Technical risks** primarily center on the profound difficulty of maintaining strict stylistic coherence across expansive catalogs of fifty or more unique graphical components using base diffusion models. This inconsistency can rapidly erode the perceived value of the platform. To mitigate this risk, the engineering team must implement strict image-prompting adapter pipelines and explicitly offer premium-tier users the computational ability to train temporary, session-specific low-rank adaptation models based exclusively on their personally uploaded reference art. Furthermore, the inherent mathematical complexity of executing unbounded state machine simulations poses a critical operational risk. The massive compute costs associated with running these simulations at scale are highly variable. This must be mitigated by introducing rigorous, queue-based throttling systems, strictly capping simulation iterations for lower-tier accounts, and aggressively investigating the compilation of the balance engine into WebAssembly to offload basic mathematical simulations directly to the client's localized browser environment, thereby bypassing centralized server costs entirely.

**Market risks** involve vocal ideological resistance from established segments within the tabletop community who actively oppose the proliferation of generative artificial intelligence, frequently labeling uncurated, low-effort outputs as "gameslop." To mitigate this perception risk, the platform must be aggressively positioned in all marketing communications as a high-fidelity prototyping and mathematical balancing utility rather than a final-art replacement service. The messaging must consistently emphasize the efficiency of the layout engine and the rigor of the mathematical simulations, focusing strictly on augmenting the capabilities of human designers rather than attempting to replace human illustrators entirely.

**Legal risks** involve highly complex, evolving disputes regarding intellectual property infringement related to the foundational training data of generative models. The platform must mitigate this exposure by establishing highly standardized terms of service that explicitly specify that individual users retain all copyright ownership over their compiled layout arrangements and original mechanical text. Furthermore, the platform must strictly utilize commercially safe, heavily curated foundational models for all premium asset generation to effectively shield enterprise users and publishers from potential downstream litigation.

## 9. Open Questions & Recommendations

1. **State Management Architecture for Simulations:** The initial repository analysis reveals the presence of legacy Node.js dependencies, specifically express and better-sqlite3, suggesting an aborted monolithic architecture approach. Is the platform legally and technically committed to a Node.js backend for the execution of complex mathematical modeling?
   - *Recommendation:* Node.js is severely suboptimal for executing the CPU-bound, multi-threaded calculations required by complex Monte Carlo simulations. The entire backend orchestration layer must be aggressively migrated to a Python-based FastAPI architecture. This allows the simulation engine to directly leverage highly optimized mathematical libraries, ensuring latency budgets remain intact. The legacy Node/Express dependencies must be purged from the repository immediately.

2. **Handling Highly Unorthodox Mechanical Concepts:** How will the rigid state machine within the Balance engine accurately simulate esoteric or physical gameplay mechanics that fall entirely outside its pre-programmed structural taxonomy?
   - *Recommendation:* The engineering team must introduce a fallback "Proxy Metric" evaluation system. If a user dictates a novel, un-simulatable mechanic—such as requiring a player to physically balance a component for ten seconds—the user interface must intercept the entity and prompt the user to map the physical action to a standardized economic proxy value within the simulation.

3. **Asset Storage and Bandwidth Economics:** The high-resolution, uncompressed TIFF files strictly required by direct manufacturing integrations will rapidly inflate centralized object storage costs. How can the platform manage this massive data footprint without degrading the required output quality?
   - *Recommendation:* The platform must enforce a strict policy of storing only base-level image generations as highly compressed WebP or JPEG formats within the persistent database. The platform should only execute the compute-heavy upscaling algorithms and required CMYK color profile conversions dynamically, directly in memory, at the exact moment the user requests a localized export or initiates a payload transmission to the manufacturing API.
